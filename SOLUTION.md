I built a deterministic, rule-based entity linking pipeline in Python. The pipeline loads all three source tables, normalizes organization names and lightweight address fields, compares records across sources, groups matched records into clusters, and repopulates `resolved.entities` plus `resolved.entity_source_links` on each run. I chose a rules-based approach because the dataset is small enough to inspect directly and the prompt emphasizes judgment, reproducibility, and explainability over model complexity.

The matcher is intentionally precision-first. Names are normalized by uppercasing, stripping punctuation, removing legal suffixes such as `Inc` and `LLC`, and expanding a few common abbreviations. Addresses are normalized at a lightweight level by standardizing street tokens, cities, states, and ZIP-5 values. Matches use a fixed weighted score over normalized name, street, city, state, and ZIP, then apply deterministic thresholds that emit a `match_method` tag such as `exact_name_state`, `exact_name_zip_city`, or `fuzzy_name_state_zip`. I also added a Roman numeral safeguard so series names like `II` and `III` do not merge.

The main trade-off was precision versus recall. I preferred missing a few real matches over collapsing unrelated entities. That is why the strongest merges require exact normalized names plus supporting geography, and the fuzzy rules still require state and ZIP or strong address agreement. The downside is that some legitimate but weaker matches may remain singletons. A second trade-off is simplicity versus address fidelity: I implemented lightweight street normalization instead of full USPS-style parsing to keep the pipeline readable and deterministic within the time box.

Three tricky examples stood out. First, `WESTPOINT INDUSTRIAL REALTY II LP` versus `Westpoint Industrial Realty III` looked similar enough to merge under a generic fuzzy rule, so I added an explicit Roman numeral mismatch check. Second, `BAKER AND HOLT INDUSTRIES INC` versus `Baker Holt Industries` required normalizing both legal suffixes and conjunction style. Third, clusters like `BEACON FEDERAL DEPT SERVICES INC` and `UNDERWOOD DEFENSE SYSTEMS INC` appear in multiple state registrations as well as SEC and USAspending data. I allowed those merges when normalized name plus geography aligned, but they remain the biggest ambiguity because multistate registrations can either represent one real company or multiple similarly named legal entities.

With another day of work, I would make the multistate matching more conservative by treating state registration rows as weaker evidence unless they also align to a non-state source, add more regression tests around those cases, and produce a small validation report showing the largest clusters and lowest-confidence non-singleton matches for manual review.
